💧 What is a Pumping Test?

A pumping test (also called an aquifer test) is a field experiment where water is pumped from a well at a controlled rate, and the resulting water-level decline (drawdown) is measured over time in the pumping well and/or nearby observation wells.

Why do we perform pumping tests?

  • Determine aquifer hydraulic properties — Transmissivity (T), Storativity (S), Hydraulic Conductivity (K), and Specific Yield (Sy)
  • Assess sustainable yield — How much water can be extracted without over-depleting the aquifer?
  • Identify boundary conditions — Detect recharge boundaries, barrier boundaries, or leakage from adjacent layers
  • Design well fields — Determine well spacing, predict interference between wells
  • Estimate radius of influence — How far does the cone of depression extend?

The Cone of Depression

When a well pumps water, the water table (or piezometric surface) drops in a cone-shaped pattern centered on the well. This is called the cone of depression. The drawdown is greatest at the pumping well and decreases with distance. The shape and rate of growth of this cone tells us about the aquifer's properties.

Types of Aquifers

TypeDescriptionKey Behavior
ConfinedSandwiched between two impermeable layers (aquitards)Water is under pressure. Drawdown responds quickly. Governed by elastic storage (S ≈ 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻³)
UnconfinedUpper boundary is the water table (free surface)Water drains from pores by gravity. Delayed yield effect. Specific yield Sy ≈ 0.01 to 0.3
Leaky (Semi-confined)Bounded by an aquitard that allows some vertical flowWater leaks through the aquitard, recharging the aquifer during pumping

🔍 Tab 3: Diagnostic Plot

This is the most powerful diagnostic tool in modern pumping test analysis — and AquiPro computes it automatically.

What is the Bourdet Derivative?

The Bourdet derivative (Bourdet et al., 1989) plots the logarithmic derivative of drawdown against time on a log-log scale. It is defined as:

\( s' = \frac{ds}{d(\ln t)} = t \cdot \frac{ds}{dt} \)

AquiPro uses the three-point weighted formula for numerical stability:

\( s'_i = \frac{\frac{\Delta s_1}{\Delta \ln t_1} \cdot \Delta \ln t_2 + \frac{\Delta s_2}{\Delta \ln t_2} \cdot \Delta \ln t_1}{\Delta \ln t_1 + \Delta \ln t_2} \)

The L parameter (smoothing factor) controls how far apart the neighboring points are when computing the derivative. Increase L if your derivative is too noisy; decrease it to capture fine detail.

Reading the Diagnostic Plot

The shape of the derivative curve reveals the aquifer type:

Confined

Flat Derivative

Derivative stabilizes as a horizontal line at late time. This is classic infinite-acting radial flow (IARF) — the aquifer behaves as a confined, infinite system.

Use: Theis or Cooper-Jacob

Unconfined

Dip and Recovery

Derivative dips below the early stabilization, then rises back. This delayed yield signature occurs as gravity drainage from the unsaturated zone catches up.

Use: Neuman

Leaky

Dropping Derivative

Derivative drops toward zero at late time. Water leaking through the aquitard stabilizes the drawdown, reducing the derivative.

Use: Hantush-Jacob

Barrier

Rising Derivative

Derivative continuously increases (doubles the IARF level). An impermeable boundary causes extra drawdown as the cone hits the barrier.

Use: Image well method or boundary-aware Theis

⚠️ Wellbore Storage: A unit slope (45° line) at early time in both drawdown and derivative indicates wellbore storage — water being released from the casing, not the aquifer. Ignore these points during curve matching or use the preprocessing flag in the Data Input tab.

📐 Tab 4: Analysis

This is where you determine the aquifer's hydraulic properties by matching your observed data to a theoretical type curve.

Choosing a Method

Select a method from the dropdown based on your diagnostic plot results or aquifer knowledge. AquiPro implements five methods (see the Theory section below for full equations).

Manual Curve Matching

Use the T and S sliders to manually adjust the theoretical curve until it overlays the observed data. The sliders are logarithmic — small movements produce large parameter changes. Watch the RMSE and R² values update in real time.

Automatic Fitting (Levenberg-Marquardt)

Click "Auto-Fit" and AquiPro will run the Levenberg-Marquardt nonlinear least-squares optimizer to find the T and S (and Sy or r/B where applicable) that minimize the sum of squared residuals between observed and theoretical drawdown.

How the Optimizer Works

  1. Starts from the current slider values as initial guesses
  2. Computes the Jacobian matrix using finite differences
  3. Iteratively adjusts parameters using a damped Gauss-Newton step
  4. Uses log-transformation to enforce positive parameter bounds
  5. Converges when improvement drops below tolerance (up to 200 iterations)
💡 Tip: If auto-fit gives unexpected results, try adjusting the sliders to give it a better starting point, then run it again. The optimizer finds a local minimum — a good initial guess helps it find the global one.

Axis Modes

  • Log-Log — Standard for Theis type-curve matching. Both axes logarithmic.
  • Semi-Log — Standard for Cooper-Jacob. Time on log axis, drawdown on linear axis.
  • Linear — Useful for checking data quality and late-time behavior.

📚 Theory & Analytical Methods

All methods in AquiPro are based on classical analytical solutions to the groundwater flow equation. Below is the theory behind each method.

The Groundwater Flow Equation

For a confined, homogeneous, isotropic aquifer with radial flow to a well, the governing equation is:

\( \frac{\partial^2 s}{\partial r^2} + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial s}{\partial r} = \frac{S}{T}\frac{\partial s}{\partial t} \)

where s is drawdown (m), r is radial distance (m), t is time (s), T is transmissivity (m²/s), and S is storativity (dimensionless).

1. Theis Method (1935)

Confined

Charles Vernon Theis derived the first transient solution for flow to a well in a confined aquifer, drawing an analogy with heat conduction.

Assumptions

  • Aquifer is confined, homogeneous, isotropic, and of infinite lateral extent
  • Well is fully penetrating and has infinitesimal diameter
  • Pumping rate Q is constant
  • Water is released instantaneously from elastic storage
  • No recharge or leakage

The Theis Equation

\( s = \frac{Q}{4\pi T} W(u) \)

where W(u) is the Theis well function (exponential integral):

\( W(u) = \int_u^{\infty} \frac{e^{-y}}{y}\,dy = -\gamma - \ln u + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1} u^n}{n \cdot n!} \)

and the dimensionless parameter u is:

\( u = \frac{r^2 S}{4 T t} \)

Here, γ = 0.5772 is the Euler-Mascheroni constant. AquiPro uses a 30-term series expansion for small u and a continued-fraction approximation for large u, ensuring at least 6 significant digits of precision.

How to Apply

  1. Plot observed s vs. t on log-log axes
  2. Overlay the theoretical W(u) vs. 1/u type curve
  3. Match the curves by adjusting T and S (or use Auto-Fit)
  4. Read off T and S from the match

2. Cooper-Jacob Method (1946)

Confined

Cooper and Jacob showed that for large values of time (when u < 0.05), the Theis well function can be approximated by its first two terms:

\( s \approx \frac{Q}{4\pi T} \left[ -0.5772 - \ln\!\left(\frac{r^2 S}{4Tt}\right) \right] = \frac{2.3\,Q}{4\pi T} \log\!\left(\frac{2.25\,Tt}{r^2 S}\right) \)

This means that on a semi-log plot (s vs. log t), the drawdown data should fall along a straight line at late time.

How to Apply

  1. Plot s vs. log(t) on semi-log axes
  2. Identify the straight-line portion (late-time data where u < 0.05)
  3. Determine the slope Δs per log cycle
  4. Compute: \( T = \frac{2.3\,Q}{4\pi\,\Delta s} \)
  5. Extend the line to s = 0 to find t₀, then: \( S = \frac{2.25\,T\,t_0}{r^2} \)
⚠️ Validity Check: Cooper-Jacob is only valid when u < 0.05. Early-time data (high u) will deviate from the straight line. Always verify using the diagnostic plot.

3. Hantush-Jacob Method (1955)

Leaky Confined

For a leaky (semi-confined) aquifer, water seeps vertically through the overlying or underlying aquitard during pumping. Hantush and Jacob derived:

\( s = \frac{Q}{4\pi T} W\!\left(u,\, \frac{r}{B}\right) \)

where W(u, r/B) is the Hantush well function:

\( W\!\left(u,\, \frac{r}{B}\right) = \int_u^{\infty} \frac{1}{y} \exp\!\left(-y - \frac{r^2}{4B^2 y}\right) dy \)

and B is the leakage factor:

\( B = \sqrt{\frac{T\,b'}{K'}} \)

where b' is the aquitard thickness and K' is the aquitard vertical hydraulic conductivity. As r/B → 0, the Hantush function reduces to the Theis function (no leakage).

How to Apply

  1. Plot s vs. t on log-log axes
  2. Select the Hantush-Jacob method in AquiPro
  3. Adjust T, S, and the r/B slider to match the curve
  4. The drawdown will plateau at late time (a key signature of leaky aquifers)

4. Neuman Method (1975)

Unconfined

In unconfined aquifers, the response to pumping occurs in two phases due to delayed yield (gravity drainage):

  • Early time: Water is released from elastic storage (like a confined aquifer). Drawdown follows a Theis curve governed by S (elastic storativity).
  • Transition: Drawdown flattens as gravity drainage begins.
  • Late time: Water drains by gravity from the declining water table. Drawdown follows a second Theis curve governed by Sy (specific yield).

Neuman's solution introduces the dimensionless parameter:

\( \sigma = \frac{S}{S_y} \)

AquiPro approximates the Neuman solution using a dual-Theis approach with a sigmoid transition between early-time (elastic) and late-time (gravity) segments, controlled by σ.

Parameters Solved

  • T — Transmissivity (from both early and late segments)
  • S — Elastic storativity (early time)
  • Sy — Specific yield (late time, typically 0.01–0.3)

5. Theis Recovery Method (1935)

Recovery

After the pump is shut off, water levels recover. The recovery data provides an independent check on the transmissivity. The residual drawdown (s') at time t after pump start (where t' = time since pump off) is:

\( s' = \frac{Q}{4\pi T} \left[ W(u) - W(u') \right] \approx \frac{2.3\,Q}{4\pi T} \log\!\left(\frac{t}{t'}\right) \)

On a semi-log plot of s' vs. t/t', the recovery data forms a straight line. The slope gives T. This method is particularly valuable because:

  • It's less affected by wellbore storage (which occurred during pumping, not recovery)
  • It provides an independent T estimate to cross-check your pumping phase analysis
  • It works even when the pumping rate was not perfectly constant

The Bourdet Derivative — A Modern Diagnostic

Introduced by Bourdet et al. (1989), the derivative method revolutionized pumping test interpretation. Rather than guessing which type curve to use, the derivative reveals the aquifer type through its characteristic shape.

Why is the derivative so powerful?

  • For a Theis model, the derivative equals \( \frac{Q}{4\pi T} \) — a constant. This means a flat derivative directly gives you T.
  • Boundary effects, leakage, and delayed yield produce distinct departures from the flat line, making them easy to identify.
  • Multiple flow regimes (wellbore storage → radial flow → boundary effects) appear as distinct segments.

🎯 Radius of Influence

The radius of influence (R) is the distance from the pumping well beyond which drawdown is negligible. AquiPro uses the Cooper-Jacob approximation:

\( R = \sqrt{\frac{2.25\,T\,t}{S}} \)

where t is the total pumping duration. This is an estimate — in reality, the cone of depression extends asymptotically.

📝 Practical Note: The ROI grows with the square root of time — doubling the pumping time only increases the ROI by about 41%. This has important implications for long-duration tests.

AquiPro also computes drawdown contour rings by solving the Theis equation inversely — finding the distance r at which a specific drawdown value (0.1, 0.5, 1, 2, 5 m) would occur.

💡 Tips & Best Practices

Data Collection

  • Record data at logarithmically spaced intervals — more frequent in early time (every 30s for the first 10 min), less frequent later (every 30 min after 6 hours)
  • Always measure the static water level before pumping starts
  • Monitor for at least 24–72 hours for reliable late-time parameters
  • Record the recovery after shutting off the pump — it provides a free second analysis

Analysis Workflow

  1. Always start with the Diagnostic Plot. Don't guess the aquifer type — let the derivative tell you.
  2. Use preprocessing wisely. Remove obvious outliers, but don't over-smooth — you may lose real signal.
  3. Try multiple methods. Compare T and S from Theis, Cooper-Jacob, and Recovery. If they agree, you have confidence in the result.
  4. Check the residuals. Random scatter means a good fit. Systematic patterns mean the model is wrong.
  5. Set a good initial guess before auto-fit. The optimizer converges faster and more reliably.
  6. Run sensitivity analysis to understand parameter uncertainty.

Common Pitfalls

  • 🚫 Ignoring wellbore storage effects in early-time data
  • 🚫 Using Cooper-Jacob when u > 0.05 (the approximation breaks down)
  • 🚫 Assuming confined conditions when the aquifer is actually unconfined
  • 🚫 Forgetting to account for partial penetration effects
  • 🚫 Using only one observation well — multiple wells give much better constraints
